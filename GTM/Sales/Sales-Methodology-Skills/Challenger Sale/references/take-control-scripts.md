# Take Control Script Library

Take Control is the third and most misread Challenger attribute. It is **not
aggression.** It's the rep's refusal to (a) discount reflexively, (b) concede
the buyer's frame, and (c) skip process steps the buyer wants to skip.

The 3 misconceptions of Taking Control (from the deck) — keep these in mind
before you reach for any script:

1. Taking Control ≠ negotiation. Most of it happens **early** in the sale —
   before there's anything to negotiate.
2. Taking Control isn't only about money. Challengers take control of three
   things: **money**, **the customer's thinking**, and **the customer's
   decision cycle**.
3. Taking Control isn't aggression. It's **constructive tension** — you
   refuse to drop the frame, but you don't insult.

Use these scripts as starting points. Tailor the wording to your voice;
keep the underlying move intact.

## Money — pricing scripts

### When the buyer pushes back on price

> "Our price reflects the outcome we deliver, and we're confident in it.
> If the number is a blocker, let's talk about **scope**, not price — what
> would we take out of the project to fit your budget?"

Why it works: re-anchors on outcome, refuses to discount, gives the buyer
something to negotiate on (scope) that doesn't break the unit economics.

### When the buyer asks for a discount "to make this easy"

> "I'd rather earn the deal on value than hand you a discount on day one.
> Walk me through what you'd need to see — quantitatively — for this to
> feel like a clear yes at full price."

### When procurement opens with "your price is high"

> "Compared to what? If we're against [Competitor X], we're priced where
> they are at half the scope. If we're against doing nothing, we're more
> expensive — but doing nothing is what's costing you [the Rational
> Drowning number]. Which comparison are we having?"

### Reflexive-discount trap (avoid this)

The conversation goes:
> Buyer: "We don't have budget for that."
> Weak rep: "What number would work?" → discount conceded.
> Challenger rep: "Tell me what 'don't have budget' means here. Is it that
> the budget exists but it's owned by someone else? Or that the spend
> hasn't been justified yet to whoever owns it?"

The first answer leads to procurement. The second leads to the EB.

### When the buyer tries to flip on you mid-deal

> "When we last spoke, we were aligned on [outcome] at [price]. Help me
> understand what's changed on your side — because nothing's changed on
> ours."

## Frame — taking control of the buyer's thinking

### Replace "What's keeping you up at night?"

The Relationship Builder opener. Cedes the frame, asks the buyer to teach
you their business. Replace with hypothesis-based opening:

> "We've worked with a number of [role]s at companies like yours, and
> we're seeing three challenges come up again and again as the most
> troubling: [1], [2], [3]. Is that what you're seeing too, or would you
> add something else to the list?"

### When the buyer agrees too enthusiastically with your insight

> "I want to make sure I'm not just confirming what you already believe.
> Where in what I just said do you actually push back?"

If they can't push back → the insight wasn't a real reframe. Rebuild before
the next meeting.

### When the buyer reframes you back

Buyer: "Actually, I think the real issue is [their original frame]."
Challenger move: don't fight it directly. Use a calibrated question (Voss):

> "How would [your frame] explain [the data point we just covered]?"

Force them to defend their frame against the evidence. They'll usually
soften.

### When you need to say "I think you're wrong" without saying it

> "Most [role]s in your seat would assume the same thing. The data we're
> seeing across [N] similar companies tells a different story — can I
> walk you through it?"

You're the one with the pattern; they're the one with the case. Use it.

## Process — taking control of the buying cycle

### Replace "Who needs to be involved?"

> "Here's how we've seen [type of company] run this kind of evaluation
> successfully — let's map your process against that, and where it differs
> we'll figure out why."

You're now the one running the evaluation. They're following.

### When the buyer says "we'll loop in InfoSec / Legal / Procurement at the end"

> "That's where most deals slip 30–60 days. I'd love to introduce you to
> our security lead this week so we can run that workstream in parallel.
> Otherwise we'll be stuck waiting for them at the worst possible moment."

(This is also straight MEDDPICC — Paper Process starts at verbal yes, not
signature.)

### When you suspect you're being kept away from the EB

> "I want to make sure we don't waste your VP's time when we get to the
> approval step. Could I get 20 minutes with [EB] now to validate that
> the framing we're working under matches how they'd think about this? If
> we're misaligned, I'd rather know now than at the end."

### Mutual Action Plan opener

> "If we're going live by [target date], let's reverse-engineer what
> that requires. Here's a draft sequence — what would you change?"

You bring the draft. They edit. The plan is now yours, jointly owned.

### When the deal goes quiet for 2+ weeks

The classic "stall" script:

> "Help me understand what changed — what was true two weeks ago that
> isn't true today?"

Force a specific answer. Vague answers ("we got busy") = you've lost
relative to a competing internal priority. Find out which one.

### When the buyer asks you to "send some info"

> "Happy to. Quick question first: what would you do with it? If it's for
> [stakeholder X], I can tailor it to what they care about — otherwise
> it'll get skimmed."

Information without context is information that gets ignored. Refuse to
ship blind.

## Foils — the most dangerous Take Control situation

A **foil** is a junior, friendly contact a passive rep gravitates toward
because they're responsive. They have no power to advance the deal, but
they consume the rep's time and create the illusion of progress.

### Identifying foils

Signs the contact is a foil:
- They schedule meetings easily but never include anyone senior.
- They forward your decks but don't comment on them internally.
- They speak in generalities about "we" and "the team" but can't name
  specific roles.
- They fail the Champion Test (won't get you the EB intro, won't share the
  evaluation criteria document).
- They're flattered by your time. Mobilizers don't flatter — they use you.

### The Foil Script

> "I really appreciate the time, and I want to be useful to you. For the
> next session to actually move things forward, I'd want to bring [type
> of insight / artifact] — but that lands very differently in front of
> [senior role]. Can we plan that one with [senior role] in the room?"

If they say yes → not a foil. Promote.
If they hedge → foil. Stop investing time until they unlock access.

### When a foil has been blocking you for 3+ weeks

The hard escalation script:

> "I've enjoyed working with you on this, but I think we owe each other
> honesty. Based on how the last several sessions have gone, I'm not sure
> there's a path to a decision that includes [senior role]. If that's
> right, I should probably step back so we're not wasting each other's
> time. If that's wrong, what would you propose we do differently in the
> next 2 weeks?"

This is uncomfortable. It's also the only thing that flips a foil situation
— either the contact escalates internally to keep you, or the situation is
revealed and you free up the cycle. **Do not stay polite.**

## Stalls — when motion stops

### "We need to think about it"

> "Of course. Help me understand the specific concern — is it the scope,
> the timing, the price, or something about how this would land
> internally? Each one of those has a different answer."

### "Send me the proposal and I'll review with the team"

> "Happy to. Two things first: who's actually on the team, and what
> question are they each going to ask when they see it? I'd rather build
> the proposal so it answers their questions, not so it just lands on
> their desk."

### "We're going to delay the decision until [Q+1]"

> "I want to take that seriously. Walk me through what specifically
> changes between now and [Q+1] that would make the decision easier.
> Because if nothing changes, the same conversation in 90 days has the
> same outcome — and the [cost of delay number] keeps running."

### "Your competitor came in cheaper"

> "Cheaper for what? When we compared [their scope] to ours, the
> equivalent line items came out within [X]%. If they're substantially
> cheaper at the same scope, I'd want to understand what they took out —
> because that's usually where the surprises are."

## When NOT to use these scripts

- **In a first meeting with a Skeptic.** They'll smell rehearsed. Be
  blunter and more direct than these — match their energy.
- **In a renewal with a customer who already loves you.** Take Control on a
  loyal customer reads as combative. Use Winning by Design's expansion
  motion instead.
- **As a substitute for a real Reframe.** A Take Control script with no
  insight underneath is just a script. The buyer can tell.

## Cross-skill notes

- These scripts pair with **Never Split the Difference** — calibrated
  questions, accusation audits, "How am I supposed to do that?" — which
  give you the mechanics for Take Control without aggression.
- The **MEDDPICC Champion test** belongs at the end of every "is this a
  foil?" check.
- The Mutual Action Plan opener is the bridge into the **MEDDPICC** Paper
  Process / Decision Process letters.
