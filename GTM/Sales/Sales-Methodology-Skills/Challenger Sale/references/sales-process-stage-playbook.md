# Challenger Sales Process — Stage-by-Stage Playbook

The Challenger model doesn't replace your CRM stages; it overlays a
specific motion on top of them. The premise is that the rep is *guiding*
the buyer through a deliberately authored conversation arc — Warmer →
Reframe → Rational Drowning → Emotional Impact → New Way → Your Solution
— while simultaneously **Taking Control** of money, the buyer's frame,
and the decision cycle.

This file is the prescriptive overlay. Each stage has:

- **Goal** — what must be true to exit the stage.
- **Prescriptive actions** — what Alex actually does.
- **Artifacts** — what gets produced or refreshed.
- **Buyer signals** — the tells that the stage worked.
- **Take Control moves** — assertiveness this stage demands.
- **Failure modes** — how reps fake this stage.
- **Exit criteria** — the gate.

Default stage map (CRM-agnostic):

| # | Stage | Challenger emphasis |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | Account selection + insight prep | Insight generation, hypothesis |
| 1 | First contact (cold/warm) | Hypothesis-based opener |
| 2 | First meeting (Warmer + Reframe) | Teach for differentiation |
| 3 | Insight validation (Rational Drowning + Emotional Impact) | Make them squirm |
| 4 | Solution alignment (A New Way + Your Solution) | Resolve uniquely to us |
| 5 | Mobilizer activation + multi-thread | Consensus across 5.4 stakeholders |
| 6 | Buying process coaching | Teach the customer how to buy |
| 7 | Pricing + procurement | Take Control of money |
| 8 | Verbal yes → paper → close | Run the paper process |
| 9 | Handoff to CS / expansion | Preserve the Mobilizer |

A Take Control track runs **in parallel** across stages 1–8. It is not
its own stage — it is a posture the rep holds throughout.

## Stage 0 — Account selection + insight prep

**Goal**: Decide *which* accounts to pursue and *what* insight you'll
lead with. Done before any outreach.

**Prescriptive actions**

- Segment by **need**, not industry / geography / size (Rule #4). Group
  accounts by the same underlying problem your insight reframes.
- Answer the **heart-and-soul question** for this segment:
  > *"What's currently costing these customers more money than they
  > realize, that only we can help them fix?"*
- Identify the **unique differentiator** the insight will resolve to
  (Rule #1 pre-flight).
- Build the **3 challenges** you'll lead with in the Warmer — backed
  by data or named peer companies, not opinions.

**Artifacts**

- Segment-level Reframe (one sentence).
- 3-challenge Warmer hypothesis.
- Differentiator map (capability → competitor gap).
- Target persona list with economic-driver snippets per role.

**Buyer signals** — n/a (pre-contact).

**Take Control moves**

- Refuse to enter a deal you can't reframe. If the segment doesn't
  produce a Reframe that resolves uniquely to you, route to a
  competitor or to a different segment.

**Failure modes**

- "Account-based" lists with no shared insight → reps reinvent every
  pitch.
- Marketing absent from insight generation → 100 reps, 100 pitches.
- Building the Reframe *after* outreach starts.

**Exit criteria**

- Reframe is one sentence and passes Rules 1–4 on paper.
- Differentiator is named, defensible, and known to the rep.

## Stage 1 — First contact (cold or warm outreach)

**Goal**: Earn a 30-minute first meeting with a senior, named stakeholder
— not a foil.

**Prescriptive actions**

- Open with **hypothesis**, not curiosity. Subject line / opening line
  is a one-sentence assertion that names a peer-pattern the buyer is
  living through.
- Replace *"I'd love to learn about your challenges"* with *"Most
  [role]s in your seat are running into [X]; here's what we're seeing
  across [N] peers."*
- Target seniority by **value-driver lens**, not by org chart. The
  right first meeting is whoever owns the P&L line the Reframe moves.
- Decline meetings with foils unless they unlock access to a senior.

**Artifacts**

- Cold-email cadence with hypothesis opener (3 touches max before
  switching channel).
- LinkedIn post pinned to the Reframe (peer-story format).
- Pre-call brief — named stakeholder, seat accountabilities, and the
  Reframe re-tailored to their role.

**Buyer signals**

- Win: "How did you know we're seeing that?" or pushback on the
  hypothesis ("Actually it's #2, not #3, that's biting us").
- Lose: silence, or worse — a junior contact replies first.

**Take Control moves**

- If the junior contact is the responder, run the Foil Script (see
  `take-control-scripts.md`). Require senior access in exchange for
  more time.

**Failure modes**

- "What's keeping you up at night?" openers (ceding the frame).
- Generic industry trend reports (informing, not teaching).
- Sequencing the senior contact at the end of the cadence rather than
  as the target.

**Exit criteria**

- 30-minute meeting on calendar with someone whose seat owns the
  Reframe's economic impact.

## Stage 2 — First meeting (Warmer + Reframe)

**Goal**: Reset the buyer's framing of the problem. Earn the second
meeting.

**Prescriptive actions**

- Open the meeting with **the Warmer** — your assessment of three peer
  challenges. Let them react. Specifically *ask them to push back*.
- Deliver the **Reframe** as one slide / one sentence. *"Most [role]s
  think [X]. Our data shows [Y] — which means the real problem isn't
  [what you thought], it's [the reframed problem]."*
- Hold for the **target reaction**: *"Huh, I'd never thought of it that
  way before."* If you get *"Yes, totally agree!"* you resonated;
  rebuild before next meeting.
- Avoid jumping to solution. Even if the buyer asks "so what do you
  guys do?" — defer with: *"I want to make sure the framing is right
  first; if I'm wrong about the problem, the solution doesn't matter."*

**Artifacts**

- The Reframe slide.
- Warmer one-pager (3 challenges + benchmark anchors).
- Pre-meeting SAFE-BOLD audit run on the Reframe.
- Post-meeting note: which of the 3 challenges the buyer added /
  amplified (this is the seam to widen in Stage 3).

**Buyer signals**

- Win: a hard question, a long pause, "say more about that," or a
  request for a follow-up with someone senior.
- Lose: enthusiastic agreement with no challenge.
- Critical: did the buyer push back? A real Reframe earns pushback. No
  pushback = no reframe.

**Take Control moves**

- Use silence. After the Reframe, do not fill the air. Let the buyer
  sit in it.
- If the buyer reframes you back, use a calibrated question: *"How
  would [your frame] explain [the data point we just covered]?"*
  rather than rebutting directly.

**Failure modes**

- Skipping the Warmer and going cold into the Reframe (reads as
  arrogance).
- Premature solution drop (collapsing Steps 2–6 into a feature pitch).
- Treating "they liked it" as a win. Liking ≠ being moved.

**Exit criteria**

- Buyer pushed back on something in the Reframe and you survived the
  pushback.
- Second meeting on calendar, ideally with one new stakeholder added.

## Stage 3 — Insight validation (Rational Drowning + Emotional Impact)

**Goal**: Make the cost of the status quo *unsurvivable* in the buyer's
mind. Convert rational understanding into emotional urgency.

**Prescriptive actions**

- Bring the **Rational Drowning** artifact: a chart, a table, or a
  number that quantifies the cost of inaction relative to the Reframe.
- The math is on the **cost of doing nothing**, not on your product's
  ROI. If the calculator returns the price of your solution, rebuild.
- Deliver the **Emotional Impact** peer story. Specific company, same
  role as the buyer, the decision they made, the consequence. Not
  generic.
- Tailor the Rational Drowning numbers to the stakeholder's economic
  lens (CFO sees dollars, COO sees throughput, CISO sees audit risk).

**Artifacts**

- Rational Drowning one-pager / slide.
- Peer-story library (see voice-and-style guide for format).
- Tailored economic-impact appendix — one row per named stakeholder
  using `tailoring-by-role.md`.

**Buyer signals**

- Win: posture shift, "wow," a new question about scope, or unprompted
  forwarding to a peer.
- Win (high): the buyer asks to bring you back to present to a peer or
  exec.
- Lose: "interesting, send me the deck."

**Take Control moves**

- If the buyer asks you to "send the slides" without scheduling next
  steps, use: *"Happy to. Quick question first — what would you do with
  it? If it's for [stakeholder X], I can tailor it; otherwise it
  skims."*
- Do not soften the numbers. SAFE Rational Drowning kills the deal
  silently.

**Failure modes**

- Skipping Emotional Impact because it "feels manipulative." Logic
  alone doesn't beat status quo.
- Generic peer story ("companies like yours"). The story has to be
  specific enough that the buyer flinches.
- Letting the buyer pivot to "so how does your product solve this?"
  before you've completed Rational Drowning.

**Exit criteria**

- Buyer has stated, in their own words, that the cost of inaction is
  unacceptable.
- Buyer has named a deadline or a forcing function (or you've named
  one and they didn't push back).

## Stage 4 — Solution alignment (A New Way + Your Solution)

**Goal**: Sell **the solution** before selling **your solution**.
Ensure that when you finally pitch your capabilities, the bridge from
Reframe → Your Solution is inevitable.

**Prescriptive actions**

- Walk through **A New Way** as a list of capabilities the buyer would
  need to act on the Reframe — described generically, as if anyone
  could provide them. Get the buyer to agree this is the work.
- Hold the line. Resist jumping to "and we do that" until the buyer
  has named the capabilities themselves.
- Then deliver **Your Solution** — point-by-point, capability ←→
  uniqueness ←→ competitor gap. Include a surprise element.
- **Pressure-test against Rule #1**: could the buyer exit at A New Way
  and buy from a competitor? If yes, the bridge wasn't tight.

**Artifacts**

- A New Way slide (capability list, generic).
- Your Solution slide (capability ↔ uniqueness ↔ proof).
- Side-by-side competitor differentiation table (1 page max).

**Buyer signals**

- Win: "OK — so what would this actually look like in practice?"
- Win (high): the buyer starts narrating internally how they'd sell
  this to peers.
- Lose: "interesting, can you send me a quote?" — they treated it as a
  feature pitch.

**Take Control moves**

- If the buyer wants to skip A New Way and go straight to pricing, use:
  *"I want to make sure we agree on what we're solving before we price
  it — otherwise the number lands wrong."*

**Failure modes**

- Compressing Steps 5 and 6 (the buyer never bought "the solution," so
  "your solution" reads as a feature pitch).
- Naming differentiators that aren't actually unique (the "100 reps,
  100 answers" failure).
- Skipping the surprise. Without it, the close of the pitch feels
  predictable and the buyer will commoditize you.

**Exit criteria**

- Buyer explicitly agrees the capability list (Step 5) is the work.
- Buyer asks for next steps tied to *evaluating* you specifically.

## Stage 5 — Mobilizer activation + multi-thread

**Goal**: Move from a single-champion deal to a **5.4-stakeholder
consensus**. Identify which contacts are Mobilizers, which are Talkers,
and ensure at least one Mobilizer is connected to the Economic Buyer.

**Prescriptive actions**

- Run the **Mobilizer diagnostic question** on every named contact:
  *"What are the top 2 things you'd need to see change in the next 12
  months?"* — log specificity scores.
- Score each contact using `mobilizer-diagnostic-worksheet.md`.
- For each Influencer, prepare a **tailored economic-impact snippet**
  per role (CFO sees cash, VP Eng sees velocity, etc.).
- Coach Mobilizers on how to sell internally — what slides to share,
  what objections to expect, what language lands with the EB.
- If your champion turns out to be a **Talker**, do not promote them
  to "champion." Find a Mobilizer behind them or escalate.

**Artifacts**

- Stakeholder map (org chart + role + classification + access status).
- Mobilizer enablement pack (1-page they can forward internally).
- Champion letter (per MEDDPICC) for the named Mobilizer.

**Buyer signals**

- Win: a Mobilizer brings you to a peer unprompted.
- Win (high): the Mobilizer cites your Reframe to a colleague in an
  email you're CC'd on.
- Lose: only one named contact, no peer involvement after 3 weeks.

**Take Control moves**

- Run the Champion Test: ask the Mobilizer to do something mildly hard
  (get you a 20-min EB slot, share the evaluation criteria doc, intro
  to InfoSec). If they can't, they're not a Mobilizer yet.
- Foil escalation script if a junior contact is gating your access.

**Failure modes**

- Champion inflation — calling a Friend a champion because they're
  responsive.
- Skeptic dismissal — writing off a Skeptic too early. Skeptics become
  the most durable advocates once convinced.
- Single-thread deals at the buying-committee stage. Single threads
  stall.

**Exit criteria**

- ≥1 Mobilizer with EB access.
- Stakeholder map covers ≥3 named contacts spanning ≥2 roles.
- Champion Test passed by the named Mobilizer.

## Stage 6 — Buying process coaching

**Goal**: **Teach the customer how to buy.** Replace passive *"who needs
to be involved?"* with prescribed sequence. Compress cycle time.

**Prescriptive actions**

- Bring a **draft Mutual Action Plan** to the buyer. Reverse-engineer
  from go-live date back to today.
- Front-load the workstreams that always slip: InfoSec, Legal,
  Procurement. Get them in the room at *verbal yes*, not at signature.
- Coach the Mobilizer on the internal narrative — what to say to the
  CFO, what to say at the steering committee, what objections to
  pre-empt.
- Name the typical decision traps for this kind of deal (RFP scope
  drift, legacy-vendor incumbent FUD, late-stage executive flip) and
  how this deal will avoid them.

**Artifacts**

- Mutual Action Plan (jointly owned, dated, with workstream owners).
- Internal champion deck (the deck the Mobilizer presents *without*
  you).
- InfoSec / Legal / Procurement intros teed up.

**Buyer signals**

- Win: the buyer edits your MAP and sends it back.
- Lose: "we'll figure out the process internally."

**Take Control moves**

- *"Here's how we've seen [type of company] run this kind of
  evaluation successfully — let's map your process against that, and
  where it differs we'll figure out why."*
- Refuse "send me the proposal and I'll review with the team" without
  knowing who's on the team and what they'll each ask.

**Failure modes**

- Letting the buyer dictate the process passively.
- Ignoring InfoSec / Legal / Procurement until end-stage. They are
  where deals lose 30–60 days.
- Treating the MAP as paperwork rather than as the deal narrative.

**Exit criteria**

- MAP signed (or at least returned with edits).
- Workstreams sequenced; InfoSec / Legal / Procurement engaged.

## Stage 7 — Pricing + procurement

**Goal**: **Take Control of money.** Refuse reflexive discounting.
Re-anchor on outcome and scope.

**Prescriptive actions**

- Anchor on the **Rational Drowning number** from Stage 3, not on
  competitor list price.
- Negotiate **scope, not price**. If the buyer can't afford full
  scope, take something out — don't shave the unit economics.
- Run procurement against the EB's prior business commitment.
  Procurement can shave the edges; they can't undo the EB's decision.
- Pre-empt the cheaper-competitor argument with a scope decomposition.

**Artifacts**

- Pricing rationale tied to outcome (1 page).
- Scope-flex options (3 tiers, all priced in line with value).
- TCO comparison vs. doing nothing (NOT vs. competitor).

**Buyer signals**

- Win: procurement asks for term-structure flexibility, not price cut.
- Lose: buyer frames you as a commodity ("just send your best price").

**Take Control moves**

- *"Our price reflects the outcome we deliver, and we're confident in
  it. If the number is a blocker, let's talk about scope, not price."*
- *"Cheaper for what? When we compared their scope to ours, the
  equivalent line items came out within X%."*
- Reflexive-discount trap response: *"Tell me what 'no budget' means
  here. Is it that the budget exists but it's owned by someone else?
  Or that the spend hasn't been justified to whoever owns it?"*

**Failure modes**

- Discounting to "make procurement easy."
- Negotiating with procurement directly without first re-anchoring
  with the EB.
- Treating procurement's opening offer as their final position.

**Exit criteria**

- Pricing accepted at acceptable margin.
- No unilateral discount; scope adjustments documented if any.

## Stage 8 — Verbal yes → paper → close

**Goal**: Convert verbal yes to signed paper without bleed.

**Prescriptive actions**

- Treat verbal yes as **the start of the paper process**, not the end
  of the deal.
- Run the Paper Process as a workstream with named owners and dates.
- Maintain the Take Control posture — buyer behavior in this stage
  predicts post-sale behavior. Soft on terms now = soft on terms later.
- Schedule the kickoff *before* the contract is signed, contingent on
  signature.

**Artifacts**

- Final MSA / Order Form.
- Counter-signed MAP with go-live date.
- Pre-kickoff agenda + attendee list.

**Buyer signals**

- Win: buyer is pushing *you* to close (because they're now invested
  in the go-live).
- Lose: silence after verbal yes for >5 business days.

**Take Control moves**

- Stall script: *"Help me understand what changed — what was true last
  Thursday that isn't true today?"*
- Mid-deal flip script: *"When we last spoke, we were aligned on
  [outcome] at [price]. Help me understand what's changed on your
  side — because nothing's changed on ours."*

**Failure modes**

- Letting the deal sit in legal for weeks without escalating.
- Conceding terms ("we'll work it out post-sale") that aren't yours to
  concede.
- Going quiet during the paper process. Silence breeds re-evaluation.

**Exit criteria**

- Signed paper.
- Kickoff scheduled.
- Mobilizer warm-introed to CS lead.

## Stage 9 — Handoff + expansion

**Goal**: **Preserve the Mobilizer.** They got you the deal; they get
you the next deal.

**Prescriptive actions**

- Run a **3-way handoff meeting** — rep, CS lead, Mobilizer. The
  Mobilizer hears the Reframe one more time so the CS team inherits
  the same narrative.
- Document the **insight context** the deal closed on — what the buyer
  reframed, what they bought "the solution" before they bought "your
  solution." This becomes the expansion north star.
- Set up a **quarterly business review cadence** that returns to the
  Reframe — not a feature update, a strategic re-anchor.
- Watch for **Mobilizer attrition**. If they leave, your champion gap
  is the #1 churn predictor.

**Artifacts**

- Handoff brief (Mobilizer profile, Reframe, decision narrative,
  expansion thesis).
- QBR template anchored on the Reframe.
- Account expansion plan (Winning by Design's Impact / Growth bowtie
  takes over here).

**Buyer signals**

- Win: Mobilizer asks for a peer intro to another team in their company
  for a parallel use case.
- Lose: Mobilizer goes silent after kickoff.

**Take Control moves**

- Refuse to let the deal narrative drop in the handoff. The CS team
  inherits the Reframe, not just the contract.

**Failure modes**

- Anonymous handoff (no Mobilizer continuity).
- CS team narrating the relationship around features instead of the
  Reframe.
- Letting the Mobilizer's promotion / departure become a churn
  surprise.

**Exit criteria**

- CS team onboarded with the Reframe context.
- Mobilizer has a relationship with someone *other* than the AE.
- Expansion thesis written down.

## The Take Control track (parallel to Stages 1–8)

Take Control is not a stage. It's a posture across the whole motion.
The 3 misconceptions to remember at every stage:

1. Take Control ≠ negotiation. Most of it happens **early**.
2. Take Control isn't only about money. It's about **money,
   thinking, and decision cycle**.
3. Take Control isn't aggression. It's **constructive tension**.

Quick check at every stage:

- Have I ceded the frame anywhere? (Reframe weakened, "we'll do it
  your way" on process.)
- Am I avoiding tension where it's productive?
- Am I selling to a Talker because they're responsive?
- Am I about to discount reflexively?

If any "yes" — pause and apply the relevant Take Control script.
