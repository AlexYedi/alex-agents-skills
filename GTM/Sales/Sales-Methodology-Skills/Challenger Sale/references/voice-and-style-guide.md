# Challenger Voice and Style Guide for Content

This guide defines how Challenger-aligned sales content sounds, looks,
and reads — across every artifact in the buyer journey: cold emails,
LinkedIn posts, Reframe slides, peer stories, ROI one-pagers,
follow-ups, internal champion decks, and proposals.

The purpose: make every piece of outbound and in-deal content sound
like the rep is **teaching for differentiation**, not asking for time
or pitching features.

The non-negotiable: every piece of content must do one of two things —

1. **Teach** something the buyer couldn't have figured out alone, that
   resolves uniquely to your differentiator.
2. **Move the deal** by Taking Control of money, frame, or process.

If a piece does neither, it's filler. Don't ship it.

---

## Voice principles

### 1. Memorable, not agreeable

> *"Be memorable, not agreeable. It's nice to have a business
> conversation about profits and capabilities, or a relationship
> conversation around sports and kids — but unless you frame your
> conversation around an edgy or unique insight, the customer will
> forget everything you said as soon as you walk out the door."*

Every artifact should pass the **forgetability test**: if the buyer
forwarded it without context, would the receiver pause? If it could
have come from any vendor in the category, rebuild.

### 2. Lead *to*, not *with*

The pitch arc — Warmer → Reframe → Rational Drowning → Emotional
Impact → A New Way → Your Solution — applies to written content too.
A blog post leads *to* the differentiator across 1,200 words. A cold
email leads *to* a meeting via one peer-pattern hypothesis.

If the first sentence of any artifact is about your product, you've
collapsed Steps 2–6.

### 3. Hypothesis voice

State assertions, not questions. Bring the frame.

| Avoid | Use |
|---|---|
| *"What's keeping you up at night?"* | *"Most CFOs in your seat are running into [X]. We're seeing it across [N] peers."* |
| *"We'd love to learn about your priorities."* | *"Here's what we're seeing across [peer set]. Tell us where we're wrong."* |
| *"How does your team currently handle this?"* | *"Most teams default to [Y], which costs them [Z]. Is that what's happening here?"* |

### 4. Constructive tension, not combat

The voice carries edge without insult. Disagreement is welcomed; the
content invites pushback. *"Tell us where we're wrong"* is the close
on most teaching content. Never *"we're right and you're wrong."*

### 5. Specific, peer-anchored, quantified

Every claim links to (a) a named or disguised peer, (b) a number or a
delta, or (c) both. Generic industry claims sound like a research
report, not a teaching conversation.

| Avoid | Use |
|---|---|
| *"Companies are seeing efficiency gains."* | *"At a 250-person FinTech we worked with last year, finance reclaimed 11 days per close cycle."* |
| *"Many teams struggle with onboarding."* | *"In a sample of 18 Series-B SaaS companies, average onboarding ran 8 weeks. The top quartile ran 3."* |

### 6. Drama, not information

Teaching content has shape: safe → dark → light → resolve. Even a
180-character LinkedIn post has it (hypothesis → tension → resolution).
Information transfer ("here are 5 trends") doesn't move buyers out of
status quo.

## Tone matrix — by audience

| Audience | Tone | What lands | What doesn't |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Executive (CEO, CFO, CRO, COO)** | Strategic, peer-confident, expensive | Pattern recognition across peers; explicit cost of inaction; reframe of what they thought the problem was | Feature lists; productivity gains; "easy to use" |
| **Influencer / End user** | Generous, expert, useful | Unique perspectives on their problem; helping them avoid landmines; ongoing advice | Talking down; treating them like a stepping stone; feature lists |
| **Procurement** | Direct, scope-anchored, calm | Total cost of ownership tied to outcome; term-structure flexibility; a clear what-you-get | Apologetic discount language; vague "value" without numbers |
| **Skeptic** | Even more direct than usual; pre-empt objections | Hard data, named peers, willingness to be wrong | Polish; rhetorical flourish; over-promise |
| **Internal champion** | Co-conspirator; equip them to sell internally | A 1-page they can forward; the words they'll use; the pre-empted internal objections | Asking them to do your work without giving them the assets |
| **Internal team (BDR, AE, CSM)** | Operational, sharp, no fluff | The Reframe in one sentence; the proof points; the Take Control scripts | Framework theory without examples |

## Style rules

### Sentence-level

- **Short sentences for the Reframe.** One assertion, one breath.
- **Numbers anchor every claim.** No naked superlatives.
- **No filler verbs.** *"We help [X]"* → *"Our customers do [X]."*
- **No hedges in the Reframe.** *"We think this might be"* drains
  authority. *"This is"* is the voice.
- **Active voice always.** *"The approach reduces..."* → *"Teams cut..."*

### Paragraph-level

- **Lead with the frame, not the company.** *"Most CFOs assume..."*
  beats *"At [Company], we believe..."*
- **One idea per paragraph.** Reading speed is the buyer's defense.
- **Cite peers, not yourself.** Self-citation is a tell of weakness.

### Document-level

- **Open with the punch.** Reframe in the first 3 lines.
- **Resolve to a unique differentiator.** Always.
- **End with a Take Control move.** Specific next step or ask, not
  *"let me know if you have questions."*

---

## Forbidden language

The following phrases are **banned** in all Challenger-aligned content.
They signal Relationship Builder voice and erode authority instantly.

- *"What's keeping you up at night?"*
- *"I'd love to learn more about your business."*
- *"Just checking in."*
- *"Circling back."*
- *"Hope this finds you well."*
- *"At [Company], we believe..."* (lead with the buyer, not you).
- *"We're a leading provider of..."* (every vendor says this).
- *"Best-in-class," "world-class," "industry-leading."*
- *"Synergy," "leverage" (as a verb), "robust."*
- *"Let me know if you have any questions."* (passive close).
- *"What would it take to earn your business?"* (cede + discount bait).

## Required language patterns

The following patterns are the **default move** in Challenger content.

### Hypothesis opener

> *"Most [role]s in your seat are running into [X]. Across [peer set],
> we're seeing [pattern]. Tell us if that matches your view."*

### Reframe statement

> *"Most [role]s think [X]. Our data shows [Y] — which means the real
> problem isn't [old frame], it's [new frame]."*

### Cost-of-inaction anchor

> *"Teams that stick with [current approach] lose [N units] per
> [period]. At your scale, that's [$X / Y days / Z headcount]."*

### Peer-story spine

> *"One [role] at a [size + segment] company we worked with [time
> frame] told us they'd been [behavior the buyer recognizes]. Here's
> what happened next: [consequence]. The reason this matters to you is
> [the precise parallel]."*

### Take Control close

> *"If [reframe] is right, the question isn't whether to act — it's
> when, and with whom. Let's put 30 minutes on the calendar to map
> what 'when' looks like at your company."*

### Constructive-pushback invitation

> *"This is the framing we'd push back on if we were in your seat:
> [counter-position]. If we're wrong, tell us where."*

## Format-specific guides

### Cold outreach email (3-touch cadence)

**Touch 1 — Hypothesis**

```
Subject: [Role] at [peer] hit [specific consequence]

Most [role]s at [segment] companies are running into [X].
Across [N] peers, we're seeing [specific pattern with number].

Tell us if that's not what's happening at [Company] — happy to
share what worked for [disguised peer], who hit the same thing
last [time period].

— [Rep]
```

**Length**: 70–110 words. **Open rate driver**: subject line names a
peer-pattern outcome, not your product.

**Touch 2 — Peer story**

```
Subject: How [disguised peer] solved [the consequence]

When [disguised peer]'s [role] hit the same wall, they went down a
similar path: [generic-sounding decision]. What it cost them: [number].

What unstuck them: [insight that resolves to your differentiator],
not [the obvious thing that didn't work].

Worth 20 minutes to walk you through what they found?

— [Rep]
```

**Touch 3 — Walk-away**

```
Subject: Stepping back

Three notes from us, then I'll stop pinging:

1. The pattern we shared (Touch 1) costs [segment] companies [$X/yr]
   on average. Worth knowing whether [Company] is exposed.
2. If it's not the right time, no read needed.
3. If we got the frame wrong, we'd want to know.

— [Rep]
```

**Banned in cold email**: any opener starting with *"I hope this finds
you well"* or *"My name is [name] and I work at [company]."*

---

### LinkedIn post (Challenger format)

**Structure**: Hook (line 1) → Tension (lines 2–4) → Reframe (line 5) →
Peer-anchored proof (lines 6–9) → Take-action ask (last line).

**Length**: 800–1,500 characters in body. First 200 chars carry it
above the fold.

**Pattern**:

```
Most [role]s think [X].

Across the last [N] [companies / deals / weeks], we've been seeing
the opposite.

Here's the pattern:
- [Observation 1]
- [Observation 2]
- [Observation 3]

The real problem isn't [old frame]. It's [new frame].

[Disguised peer] figured this out 6 months ago. Here's what changed:
[specific delta tied to a number].

Where would you push back on this?

— [Rep]
```

**Voice rules**

- No emojis except sparingly to separate visual groups (rep's call).
- No hashtag spam. 1–3 max, all relevant.
- Tag the peer or named source if you have permission. Otherwise
  disguise rigorously.
- Close with an invitation to disagree, not with a CTA to your demo.

### Reframe slide (executive deck)

**Spec**: 1 slide. 1 sentence. 1 supporting visual. No more.

**Layout**:

```
┌───────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ Most [role]s think [X].                   │
│                                           │
│ Our data shows [Y].                       │
│                                           │
│ The real problem: [new frame].            │
│                                           │
│ ────────                                  │
│                                           │
│ [One peer-anchored chart]                 │
└───────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Test**: cover the rest of the deck. Could a competitor present this
slide and win the room? If yes — rebuild.

---

### Rational Drowning one-pager

**Spec**: 1 page. 1 number. 1 chart. 1 named peer.

**Required components**

1. **Headline number** — the cost of inaction in the buyer's units.
2. **Peer benchmark** — what the top quartile of comparable companies
   gets to.
3. **Cost-decomposition chart** — where the dollars actually live.
4. **Unit math** — how the buyer can calculate their own exposure
   from inputs they already know.
5. **No reference to your product**. Anywhere.

**Test**: hand it to a friend who knows nothing about your company.
They should leave thinking *"this is bad, we have to do something"* —
NOT *"this is interesting, what does this company sell?"*

---

### Emotional Impact peer story (1 paragraph)

**Spec**: 80–140 words. Specific company (named or disguised). Specific
role. Specific decision. Specific consequence. Specific parallel to
the buyer.

**Template**:

```
At [disguised peer — give size + segment, not name if confidential],
the [same role as the person reading] decided [the seemingly
reasonable decision the buyer is also leaning toward]. It looked
right at the time — [reason it looked right].

Six months in: [the consequence, quantified].

What makes this matter for [Buyer Company]: [the precise parallel —
same decision pattern, similar exposure].
```

**Voice**: matter-of-fact. No melodrama. Specificity does the
emotional work; embellishment kills it.

---

### Mobilizer enablement 1-pager

**Audience**: the named Mobilizer who'll forward this internally
without you in the room.

**Spec**: 1 page. Should answer: *"Why are you sending me this, and
what should I do with it?"*

**Required sections**

1. **The Reframe** (1 sentence).
2. **The cost of inaction** (1 number with unit).
3. **What the next step is** (specific — *"a 30-min session with [VP
   X]"*, not *"learn more"*).
4. **Pre-empted objections** — what the CFO / CISO / Procurement will
   ask, with 1-line answers the Mobilizer can use.
5. **Why us, in 3 lines** — the differentiator, named.

**Voice**: speak through the Mobilizer's voice, not the rep's. They
are forwarding this as if they wrote it. Match their register.

---

### Follow-up email (post-meeting)

**Spec**: 80–150 words. Sent within 4 hours of the meeting. Not a
recap.

**Required components**

1. **One sentence** that echoes a specific thing the buyer said —
   proves you listened.
2. **The Reframe**, restated in one sentence.
3. **One named next step** with a date.
4. **One artifact** attached or linked, tailored to what the buyer
   reacted to most strongly.

**Banned**: bullet-point recaps of what was discussed (the buyer was
there). Generic "great meeting" intros. Any sentence starting with
*"As we discussed."*

---

### Mutual Action Plan opener (Stage 6)

**Spec**: 1 page. Reverse-engineered from go-live date back to today.
Workstreams, owners, dates.

**Voice**: collaborative authorship, but the rep brought the draft.

**Header line**:

> *"If we're going live by [date], here's the sequence we've seen work
> at [N] similar companies. What would you change?"*

The buyer edits the document. The plan becomes jointly owned. **You**
brought it.

---

### Internal pitch deck (the rep's presentation deck)

**Order matters**. The deck enforces the choreography.

| Slide | Content |
|---|---|
| 1 | Title — the Reframe as the title |
| 2 | Warmer — 3 peer challenges with benchmark anchors |
| 3 | Reframe — 1 sentence, 1 visual |
| 4 | Rational Drowning — cost-of-inaction with peer chart |
| 5 | Emotional Impact — peer story (1 slide, 1 paragraph) |
| 6 | A New Way — capability list (vendor-agnostic) |
| 7 | Your Solution — capability ↔ uniqueness ↔ proof |
| 8 | Tailored economic-impact appendix (1 row per stakeholder) |
| 9 | Next steps — Mutual Action Plan draft |

**Slide-count discipline**: if the deck creeps past 12 slides, it's
collapsing into a feature pitch.

---

### Proposal / order form cover letter

**Spec**: 1 page. Voice is calm, confident, scope-anchored.

**Required components**

- **Outcome statement** (the Reframe's promise restated).
- **Scope** — what's in / what's out.
- **Pricing rationale** — anchored on outcome, not on competitor.
- **Mutual obligations** — what we commit to, what they commit to.
- **No discount language** in the cover letter. Discounts (if any)
  live in the order form, with rationale.

## Voice failure modes (what to catch in review)

Run any draft against this checklist before shipping:

| Failure | Tell |
|---|---|
| Relationship Builder voice | Curious openings, hedges, "love to learn" |
| Lead-with-product | First sentence names your product or company |
| SAFE (not BOLD) | Could be delivered at an industry conference with no pushback |
| Generic | Could be sent unchanged to any account in the segment |
| Resonance trap | Reads like the buyer would nod along — no reframe |
| Forgettable | Opens with formalities; no specific number until line 5+ |
| Foil-friendly | Asks for time without naming a senior stakeholder |
| Discount-baited | Mentions price flexibility without value anchor |
| Unowned next step | Closes with *"let me know"* instead of *"here's the next move"* |

A draft that hits 2+ of these failure modes goes back. A draft that
hits 4+ is a Relationship Builder draft and needs to be rewritten from
the Reframe down.
