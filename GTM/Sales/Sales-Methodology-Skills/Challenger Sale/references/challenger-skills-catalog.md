# Challenger Sales Skills Catalog

The Challenger framework requires a specific set of micro-skills. Reps
who absorb the framework intellectually but don't drill the skills
revert to feature-pitching under pressure. This catalog is the rep-side
training surface — what each skill is, what good looks like, what bad
looks like, and a drill to sharpen it.

For each skill:

- **Definition** — what the skill is.
- **When to use** — sales-process trigger.
- **What good looks like** — observable rep behavior + observable buyer
  reaction.
- **What bad looks like** — the failure mode it gets faked as.
- **Drill** — a practice prompt Alex can run with a coach, peer, or
  alone with a recording.

---

## 1. Hypothesis-based selling

**Definition**: Open conversations with **assertions** (peer-pattern
hypotheses) instead of curiosity questions. The rep brings the frame;
the buyer reacts.

**When to use**: Stage 1 cold outreach, Stage 2 first meeting opener,
the start of every executive call.

**What good looks like**

- Opener: *"Most [role]s in your seat are running into [X]; we're
  seeing it across [N] peers."*
- Buyer pushes back, amplifies, or adds a fourth challenge — meaning
  they engaged with the hypothesis.
- Rep treats the pushback as data, not as objection.

**What bad looks like**

- *"What's keeping you up at night?"* (Relationship Builder cede.)
- Soft hedging: *"We thought you might be running into..."* (drains
  authority).
- Hypothesis presented as a generic industry trend — no peer-pattern
  spine.

**Drill**

Take 5 named accounts. Write a one-sentence hypothesis-based opener
for each, citing the peer pattern. Read each aloud — if it sounds like
a question, rebuild as an assertion.

## 2. Reframe construction

**Definition**: Build a one-sentence statement that connects the
buyer's accepted challenges (Warmer) to a **bigger problem or bigger
opportunity** they hadn't seen — and that resolves uniquely to your
differentiator.

**When to use**: Stage 2. The single most important moment of the pitch.

**What good looks like**

- One sentence, structured: *"Most [role]s think [X]. Our data shows
  [Y] — which means the real problem isn't [old frame], it's [new
  frame]."*
- Survives the **competitor test**: if a competitor's rep could deliver
  it without awkwardness and win, it's wrong.
- Earns the *"Huh, I'd never thought of it that way"* reaction —
  including pushback.

**What bad looks like**

- *"Yes, I totally agree!"* — the resonance trap. You found pain; you
  didn't reframe.
- Reframe that resolves to "the category" rather than to your unique
  capability.
- Reframe that confirms the buyer's existing belief, just in fancier
  language.

**Drill**

Take any current Reframe and run it through the heart-and-soul
question: *"What's currently costing our customers more money than
they realize, that only we can help them fix?"* If the answer doesn't
resolve to a unique differentiator, rebuild.

## 3. Rational drowning math

**Definition**: Quantify the **cost of inaction** relative to the
Reframe. The math is on the problem, not on the product. ROI
calculators that return product ROI are the failure mode.

**When to use**: Stage 3 (Rational Drowning step in the pitch).

**What good looks like**

- A specific metric (dollars, throughput, days, headcount) tied to a
  named peer benchmark.
- A visual that lands in 10 seconds.
- Buyer reaction: *"Wow. I had no idea we were leaving that much on the
  table."*

**What bad looks like**

- A calculator that asks the buyer for their input volume and returns
  "your savings would be $X with our tool."
- A vague "industry estimate" with no peer comparison.
- A number so big the buyer dismisses it as marketing.

**Drill**

For your most common buyer persona, build the cost-of-inaction
calculator in 1 page. The math must use only inputs the buyer already
knows. Strip out every reference to your product. If you can't, the
calculator is wrong.

## 4. Emotional impact storytelling

**Definition**: Tell a specific peer-company story that makes the buyer
**feel** the pain. Logic alone doesn't beat status quo. Gut does.

**When to use**: Stage 3, immediately after Rational Drowning. Logic
sets up the story; the story converts.

**What good looks like**

- Specific named (or disguised) peer company.
- Same role at that company as the person in the room.
- The decision they made (that looked reasonable at the time).
- The consequence — quantified, painful, recent.
- Buyer reaction: silence, posture shift, a harder question.

**What bad looks like**

- *"Companies that don't adapt fail."* Generic. Skipped.
- *"A customer of ours saved 30%."* Wrong story (success, not pain).
- A story so close to the buyer's company that it reads as a threat.

**Drill**

Write 3 peer stories. For each: name the role, the decision, the
consequence, the parallel to the buyer's situation. Read each aloud.
If you don't flinch a little when you say it, it's not specific
enough.

## 5. Constructive tension management

**Definition**: Hold productive tension without softening or escalating
to combat. Comfort with silence, ambiguity, and the buyer's discomfort
is the visible behavior.

**When to use**: Throughout, but especially Stages 2, 3, 7.

**What good looks like**

- Pause for 5+ seconds after delivering the Reframe.
- Lets a hard question hang rather than rushing to answer.
- Doesn't apologize for the Rational Drowning number.
- Tells the buyer they're wrong without saying *"you're wrong."*

**What bad looks like**

- Filling silence with *"so... yeah... anyway..."* and weakening the
  Reframe.
- Apologizing for being assertive (*"sorry, just to push back gently
  here..."*).
- Premature closure — rushing to resolve a tension that should have
  stayed open.

**Drill**

Record yourself delivering a Reframe. Time the silence after. Aim for
5+ seconds. Most reps cut to 1–2.

## 6. Take Control of price

**Definition**: Refuse reflexive discounting. Re-anchor on outcome,
negotiate scope rather than price, route procurement through the EB's
prior decision.

**When to use**: Stage 7 and any earlier price probe.

**What good looks like**

- Anchors on the Rational Drowning number, not on competitor list.
- Default response to "your price is high": *"Compared to what?"*
- Scope-flex options before discount.

**What bad looks like**

- *"What number would work?"* (immediate concession).
- Apologizing for the price.
- Discounting to "make procurement easy."

**Drill**

Roleplay 5 price objections with a peer. Use only Take Control scripts.
After each, identify: did I concede the frame, the scope, or neither?

---

## 7. Take Control of process

**Definition**: Teach the customer how to buy. Bring the Mutual Action
Plan; sequence InfoSec / Legal / Procurement; refuse passive
*"who needs to be involved?"*

**When to use**: Stage 6.

**What good looks like**

- Rep brings the MAP draft; buyer edits it.
- InfoSec / Legal / Procurement engaged at verbal yes, not at signature.
- Decision traps named in advance, not discovered at the end.

**What bad looks like**

- *"What does your typical process look like?"* (cede).
- Discovery of new approvers in the final week.
- MAP treated as paperwork, not narrative.

**Drill**

For a current open deal, draft a 1-page MAP from go-live backward.
Identify every workstream and owner. Where do you not know the owner?
That's the gap to close this week.

## 8. Take Control of the buyer's thinking

**Definition**: Refuse to concede the frame. When the buyer reframes
you back, hold ground without combat. Use the data, the pattern, the
peer.

**When to use**: Throughout, especially when the buyer pushes back on
the Reframe.

**What good looks like**

- *"How would [your frame] explain [the data point]?"* — calibrated
  question forces them to defend their frame.
- *"Most [role]s in your seat would assume the same thing. The data
  across [N] similar companies tells a different story — can I walk
  you through it?"*
- Holds the Reframe even when the buyer pushes hard.

**What bad looks like**

- Capitulating: *"That's a fair point, you might be right."*
- Combat: *"No, you're wrong about that."*
- Restating the Reframe louder.

**Drill**

Take one of your Reframes. Have a peer roleplay 3 pushback responses.
For each, draft a calibrated question response. None of the responses
may include *"yes, but"* or *"no, you're wrong."*

## 9. Foil identification

**Definition**: Recognize when a junior, friendly contact is consuming
your time without advancing the deal. Require expanded access in
exchange for more time.

**When to use**: Stage 5 — and any time a contact is too easy.

**What good looks like**

- Tracks the Champion Test outcome explicitly per contact.
- Asks: *"For the next session to actually move things, I'd want to
  bring [insight] — but it lands very differently in front of [senior
  role]. Can we plan that one with [senior role] in the room?"*
- Walks away from foil-only access.

**What bad looks like**

- Continuing to invest weeks in a foil because they're responsive.
- Treating their access as champion access.
- Calling a Friend a champion in the forecast call.

**Drill**

For each open deal, list every named contact and run the Champion Test
prompt: *"Have I asked this person to do something mildly hard, and
did they do it?"* If "no" — they haven't proven Mobilizer status yet.

## 10. Mobilizer activation

**Definition**: Identify the Mobilizers (Go-Getter / Teacher / Skeptic)
and equip them to sell internally. Do not rely on a single Champion;
build consensus across 5.4 stakeholders.

**When to use**: Stage 5.

**What good looks like**

- Mobilizer diagnostic question run on every contact: *"What are the
  top 2 things you'd need to see change in the next 12 months?"*
- Mobilizer-enablement pack the contact can forward without you.
- Mobilizer cites your Reframe in an internal email.

**What bad looks like**

- Single-thread deals at the buying-committee stage.
- Promoting a Talker to Champion because they're responsive.
- Skeptic written off because they push back.

**Drill**

For your top 3 deals, classify every named contact (Go-Getter /
Teacher / Skeptic / Friend / Guide / Climber / Blocker) using
`mobilizer-diagnostic-worksheet.md`. If you have <1 Mobilizer per
deal, that's the next sprint's work.

## 11. Tailoring by economic lens

**Definition**: Re-thread the same insight through each stakeholder's
economic accountability. CFO sees cash; VP Eng sees velocity; CISO
sees audit risk; COO sees throughput.

**When to use**: Stages 3, 4, 5.

**What good looks like**

- Per-stakeholder snippet: 1-line role driver + 1-line reframe
  translation + 1-line economic impact in their units.
- Tailored economic-impact appendix slide circulated by the Mobilizer.
- The same Reframe lands differently for 4 different stakeholders.

**What bad looks like**

- Same pitch deck, same numbers, all stakeholders.
- Talking dollars to a CISO (who measures audit risk).
- Talking SLA throughput to a CFO (who measures cash).

**Drill**

Take your current Reframe. Write the 3-line economic-impact snippet
for 4 different roles. If 2+ snippets sound the same, you haven't
actually tailored — you've translated.

## 12. Avoiding premature closure

**Definition**: The discipline of *not* resolving tension when the
buyer wants to. Closing too early collapses the choreography back
into a feature pitch.

**When to use**: Mid-pitch (Steps 2–5), late-stage negotiation.

**What good looks like**

- When the buyer asks "so what does your product do?" mid-Reframe:
  *"I want to make sure the framing is right first; if I'm wrong about
  the problem, the solution doesn't matter."*
- Lets objections sit on the table for several minutes longer than the
  buyer expects.
- Doesn't conflate "buyer is nodding" with "buyer is bought in."

**What bad looks like**

- Jumping from Reframe to product features because the buyer leaned in.
- Resolving an open price objection with a discount within the same
  meeting.
- Closing the call early because the energy felt right.

**Drill**

For your last 3 customer calls, identify one moment where you closed
prematurely. What would the alternative move have been?

## 13. Champion test execution

**Definition**: Ask the named Mobilizer to do something mildly hard
that proves they'll spend political capital for you. The result is
the only true signal.

**When to use**: Stage 5, before classifying a contact as a Champion in
forecast.

**What good looks like**

- Specific ask: get a 20-minute slot with the EB, share the evaluation
  scorecard, intro to InfoSec, forward the Reframe deck to a peer.
- Result tracked: did they do it, quickly, slowly, or not at all?
- Failure to pass = not a Champion yet, regardless of how friendly.

**What bad looks like**

- Asking only for things they were going to do anyway.
- Treating "yes I'll get to it" as a pass.
- Avoiding the test because you don't want to risk the relationship.

**Drill**

For every deal in your forecast that has a "Champion," list the
Champion Test you've run and the result. If you can't, run one this
week.

## 14. Pre-call hypothesis preparation

**Definition**: Walk into every meeting with a written hypothesis about
what the buyer believes, what's costing them money, and which
differentiator the Reframe must resolve to. The hypothesis is the
plan; the meeting refines it.

**When to use**: Before every external meeting from Stage 2 onward.

**What good looks like**

- 1-page pre-call brief: stakeholder, accountabilities, current belief,
  Reframe, target reaction, Take Control risks.
- The brief gets refined after the meeting based on what the buyer
  actually said.
- Same brief reused (and improved) across similar accounts.

**What bad looks like**

- Walking into a meeting "to learn about their priorities."
- Using the same generic deck across all accounts.
- No post-meeting update to the hypothesis.

**Drill**

For your next 5 customer meetings, write the 1-page pre-call brief.
After each meeting, score: did my hypothesis hold? Where did it break?

## 15. Two-way dialogue and debate

**Definition**: A teaching pitch is not a monologue. The Challenger
draws the buyer into the conversation, invites pushback, and treats
disagreement as productive.

**When to use**: Stages 2–4 in particular.

**What good looks like**

- Frequent prompts: *"Where am I wrong about this?"* / *"What would
  you push back on?"*
- The buyer talks at least 40% of the meeting (without it becoming
  discovery theater).
- Debate is welcomed; the rep doesn't deflect.

**What bad looks like**

- Solo presentation of all 6 steps without buyer interjection.
- Interrupting buyer pushback to defend the Reframe.
- Collapsing into Q&A after slide 3.

**Drill**

Record a 30-min sales meeting. Count: how many open prompts did you
ask? Aim for 1 every 3–4 minutes.

## How to use this catalog

- Tag each skill against the **stages where it applies** (see
  `sales-process-stage-playbook.md`).
- Pick **2 skills per quarter** to drill — don't try to fix all 15 at
  once.
- Pair drills with the **Mobilizer diagnostic** and **SAFE-BOLD
  audit** as the two persistent quality checks.
- Use the **agents/** in this folder when you want a coach to
  pressure-test specific skills on a real deal.
