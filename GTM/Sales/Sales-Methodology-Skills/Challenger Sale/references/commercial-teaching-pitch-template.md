# Commercial Teaching Pitch — Fill-in-the-Blank Template

Use this template to build a 6-step teaching pitch. Each step has a goal, a
target buyer reaction (the "tell" that the step worked), and a failure mode
(the "tell" that it didn't).

Rule before you start: **answer the heart-and-soul question first.**

> *"What's currently costing our customers more money than they realize,
> that only we can help them fix?"*

If you can't answer that in one sentence, stop. No choreography will save a
pitch that doesn't have a real insight underneath.

## Step 1 — The Warmer

**Goal**: Build credibility. Signal "I understand your world."

Do NOT open with *"What's keeping you up at night?"* — that cedes the frame
and reads as Relationship Builder.

**Fill in:**

> "We've worked with a number of [role]s at [similar-type companies], and
> we're seeing three challenges come up again and again as the most
> troubling:
>
> 1. ____________________________________________
> 2. ____________________________________________
> 3. ____________________________________________
>
> Is that what you're seeing too, or would you add something else to the
> list?"

**Target reaction**: "Yeah, that's about right — though #2 is bigger than
you made it."

**Failure mode**: "Well, let me tell you what's actually keeping us up at
night..." (You didn't establish credibility; now you're doing discovery.)

---

## Step 2 — The Reframe

**Goal**: Introduce a perspective that connects the Warmer challenges to a
**bigger problem** or **bigger opportunity** than the buyer realized.

This is the pitch. Everything before it earns the right to reframe.
Everything after it pays off the reframe.

**One-sentence Reframe**:

> "Most [role] think [X]. Our data shows [Y] — which means the real problem
> isn't [what you thought], it's [the reframed problem]."

My Reframe: ___________________________________________________________

**Target reaction**: *"Huh. I'd never thought of it that way before."*

**Failure mode**: *"Yes, totally agree! That's exactly what we're seeing."*
(You resonated. You didn't reframe. Rebuild.)

**SAFE-BOLD check on the Reframe**:

| Dimension | My reframe is... |
|---|---|
| **Big** — expansive, further-reaching than incremental | BOLD / SAFE |
| **Innovative** — pushes the envelope | BOLD / SAFE |
| **Risky** — asks the buyer to take real risk | BOLD / SAFE |
| **Difficult** — hard enough that it requires outside help | BOLD / SAFE |

If any SAFE → rebuild before the meeting.

---

## Step 3 — Rational Drowning

**Goal**: Quantify the cost of the problem (or size of the opportunity).
Data, graphs, tables. Make the buyer *squirm*.

**Key rule**: the math is on the **cost of inaction** relative to the
Reframe — NOT on the cost of your product. If your calculator returns ROI
on your solution, you're feature-pitching. Rebuild.

**Fill in:**

- **Specific metric that shows the cost**: ___________________________
- **Benchmark number from peer companies**: __________________________
- **This buyer's likely exposure** (even rough order of magnitude):
  _______________________________________________________________
- **Visual**: chart / table / graph that lands in 10 seconds.

**Target reaction**: *"Wow. I had no idea we were leaving that much on the
table."*

**Failure mode**: *"Interesting — can you send me the slides?"* (Not
drowning. They're not feeling it yet.)

---

## Step 4 — Emotional Impact

**Goal**: Make the buyer *feel* the pain. Logic alone doesn't beat status
quo. Gut does.

Paint a specific peer story. Generic doesn't land.

**Fill in:**

- **Peer company** (real, named if possible, disguised if not):
  _______________________
- **The role at that company** (same role as the person in front of you):
  _______________________
- **The decision they made** (that looked reasonable at the time):
  _______________________
- **The consequence** (quantified, specific, painful):
  _______________________

**Script pattern**:

> "One [role] at a company about your size told me six months in that they'd
> [specific behavior the buyer is ALSO doing]. Here's what happened next:
> [consequence]. What makes me bring this up is [the precise parallel to
> your buyer]."

**Target reaction**: *[Pause.]* Shift in posture. A harder question.

**Failure mode**: *"That's interesting, but we're different because..."*
(Story wasn't specific enough to their situation.)

---

## Step 5 — A New Way

**Goal**: Sell **the solution** (the new way of operating), not **your
solution**. Get buy-in on *behaving differently* before you pitch *buying
from you*.

> *Before they buy your solution, they have to buy the solution.*

**Capabilities the buyer would need to act differently** (framed
generically — could come from anyone):

1. ____________________________________________
2. ____________________________________________
3. ____________________________________________
4. ____________________________________________

**Rule**: resist the urge to jump to Step 6 here. The buyer is nodding —
that's the trap. If you jump to Step 6 now, Step 5 collapses and you're in
a feature pitch.

**Target reaction**: *"OK — so what would this actually look like in
practice?"*

**Failure mode**: *"So what does your product do?"* (You lost them. They
jumped to Step 6 because Step 5 wasn't specific enough.)

---

## Post-pitch audit — the 4 Rules

- [ ] **Rule #1 — Lead to unique strengths.** Does the pitch inevitably
      resolve to us? Competitor-test passed?
- [ ] **Rule #2 — Challenge assumptions.** Did the buyer's worldview shift?
      Any "never thought of it that way" moments?
- [ ] **Rule #3 — Catalyze action.** Is the ROI math on the reframe, not on
      the product?
- [ ] **Rule #4 — Scale across customers.** Is this insight reusable across
      similar accounts, or bespoke to one deal?

All four pass → ship it. Any fail → rebuild.

---

## Final polish checklist

- [ ] Emotional arc present (safe → dark → light → resolve to you).
- [ ] Both brain sides engaged (Rational Drowning + Emotional Impact).
- [ ] No premature closure — tension held through Step 5.
- [ ] Bridge from Reframe → Your Solution feels inevitable.
- [ ] Would you bet your commission on this landing in front of the CFO?
