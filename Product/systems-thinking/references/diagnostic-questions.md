# Diagnostic Questions for Systems Analysis

A working bank of questions, organized by where you are in an analysis. Pull from this file when working through any complex problem — multi-stakeholder decisions, chronic dysfunction, second-order risk, organizational redesign, or just "why does this keep happening?"

The discipline: **don't move to "what to do" until you've worked through "what's actually happening here."** Most failed interventions skip diagnosis.

Distilled from Meadows ch. 1-7 + the appendices, plus questions implicit in the leverage points and archetypes.

---

## Phase 1 — Bound the system

Before analyzing anything, agree on what's in and what's out.

- What system are we actually talking about? What's the smallest box that contains the dynamics we care about?
- What are the parts of this system?
- What are the interconnections holding the parts together — physical, informational, cultural?
- What is the system's *function or purpose* — not as stated, but as deduced from its actual behavior?
- Who is *inside* the boundary, and who is *outside* it?
- What boundary did we draw and why? What changes if we drew it differently?
- Whose definition of "the system" are we using? Whose are we ignoring?

The Sufi-elephant trap (Meadows' opening): we each see one part. Assume your boundary is incomplete; ask others how they'd draw theirs.

---

## Phase 2 — Identify stocks and flows

Sketch the structure before debating the dynamics.

- What is accumulating in this system? (These are the stocks.)
- What is moving in and out of those stocks? (These are the flows.)
- For each stock, what is its current level vs. its desired level vs. its capacity?
- For each flow, what controls its rate? Is the rate stable, accelerating, decelerating?
- Where are the buffers — the stocks that smooth out shocks?
- Are any of those buffers being drained or "optimized away"?
- Are the inflows and outflows of any critical stock currently in dynamic equilibrium, or is the stock drifting?
- Which resources here are stock-limited (nonrenewable) and which are flow-limited (renewable)? Are we managing each correctly?

If you can't sketch stocks and flows on a whiteboard in 20 minutes, you don't understand the system yet.

---

## Phase 3 — Identify feedback loops

Where does the system loop back on itself?

- What are the balancing (B) loops? What are they trying to keep stable?
- What are the reinforcing (R) loops? What are they driving toward growth or collapse?
- Where are the delays in those loops? Are they short, long, or unknown?
- Which loops dominate behavior right now? Which dominated 6 months ago?
- Which loops will dominate 6 months from now if nothing changes?
- Is any reinforcing loop currently running unchecked, with no balancing loop big enough to counteract it?
- Are any balancing loops being weakened by being "stripped of redundancy"?
- Are any feedback signals delayed, biased, or missing entirely?

Senge's lens: behavior over time *is* the feedback structure. If you graph the metric over months, the shape (linear / exponential / S-curve / oscillating) tells you which loop architecture is producing it.

## Phase 4 — Test for archetype shape

Match the situation against the eight named patterns. (See `system-archetypes.md` for full descriptions.)

- **Policy Resistance:** Does every intervention seem to be undone by other actors? Does effort produce stasis?
- **Tragedy of the Commons:** Is there a shared resource where each user benefits but the cost lands on others?
- **Drift to Low Performance:** Are standards quietly relaxing? Is "good" today defined as "what we did last quarter"?
- **Escalation:** Is there a competition where each side's response provokes the other?
- **Success to the Successful:** Are winners systematically getting more advantages for the next round?
- **Shifting the Burden:** Are we using a quick fix that's masking the underlying problem? Is the system's own capacity to solve the problem atrophying?
- **Rule Beating:** Are actors gaming the rules in ways that defeat the purpose?
- **Seeking the Wrong Goal:** Is the metric perfect but the underlying outcome worse?

If you can name the archetype, half the diagnosis is done.

---

## Phase 5 — Identify the players and their incentives

Borrowed from Sriram Krishnan's "think of all the players in the system, think of all of their incentives" — and reinforced by Meadows' Bounded Rationality chapter.

- Who are all the actors with stakes in this system?
- What does each actor want? (Not what they say they want — what their behavior shows they want.)
- What information does each actor have access to? What information do they *not* have?
- What rules govern each actor's behavior?
- What incentives drive each actor's behavior? Are the incentives aligned with the system's purpose?
- Where are actors making "rational" decisions inside their bounded view that are damaging the whole system?
- Whose goals are being overridden by current policy?
- Whose goals would need to be heard for an intervention not to trigger policy resistance?

---

## Phase 6 — Locate leverage points

Once you understand the structure, find the places to push.

- What's the highest leverage point we can actually act on? (See `leverage-points.md`.)
- Are we currently pushing on a leverage point — and pushing in the wrong direction?
- Are we putting 95% of our energy into parameters (#12) when the leverage is at rules (#5) or goals (#3)?
- Is there a missing information flow (#6) that would change everything if we restored it?
- Are the goals of the system aligned with the actual outcome we want?
- Is the paradigm underneath the goals still serving us, or has the world changed around it?

The danger: most leverage points are counter-intuitive. The intuitive intervention is often correctly identifying the leverage point and pushing the *wrong direction*.

---

## Phase 7 — Test second-order effects

Before committing to an intervention.

- If we make this change, what happens immediately? (First-order effect.)
- What happens after that, as the system responds? (Second-order.)
- What happens after that, as actors adapt their behavior to the new conditions? (Third-order.)
- Who benefits? Who's hurt? Who hasn't yet noticed?
- Are we creating a new feedback loop? What kind — reinforcing or balancing?
- Are we removing an existing balancing loop? What did that loop used to handle?
- Are we shifting the burden somewhere else?
- What new system trap could we be setting up?
- What surprises does the system have that we haven't yet seen?

The pre-mortem version: *imagine this intervention spectacularly fails; what went wrong?* The answer is usually a second-order effect we hadn't accounted for.

---

## Phase 8 — Stay humble — check the conduct

Before acting, check yourself against the Dancing-with-Systems guidelines (see `dancing-with-systems.md`).

- Have I watched the system long enough to see its rhythm, or am I jumping to interventions?
- Have I exposed my mental model to other people for challenge?
- Am I respecting and routing information honestly?
- Am I paying attention to what is important, not just what is quantifiable?
- Have I located responsibility within the system, or am I exporting it?
- Am I designing for resilience and self-organization, or just for productivity?
- Have I expanded the time horizon? The boundary of caring?
- Am I willing to be wrong and update?

When you can answer these honestly, you've earned the right to intervene.

---

## Quick-reference compact version

When time is short, use this compressed sequence:

1. **What's the system?** Boundary, parts, interconnections, purpose-as-deduced.
2. **What's accumulating?** Stocks. What's moving? Flows.
3. **What loops back?** R loops, B loops, where the delays are.
4. **What pattern is this?** Match to the 8 archetypes.
5. **Who are all the players?** Goals, incentives, information access.
6. **Where's the leverage?** Highest workable point on the 12.
7. **What breaks if we push?** Second-order effects.
8. **Can I be wrong?** Posture check.

If you only have 10 minutes: do steps 1, 2, 3, and 4. If you have 30 minutes: add 5 and 6. Always do 7 before committing.
